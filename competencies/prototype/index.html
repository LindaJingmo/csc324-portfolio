Prototyping

April 26, 2026 · 2 min · Linda Jing

1. What design questions did you intend to answer with your prototype(s)?#

Through this prototype, our group aimed to explore several key design questions:

  • How to make degree requirements more visible and understandable?
  • How to help students compare and organize courses across multiple years?
  • Whether integrating requirement tracking (e.g., major, concentration, credits taken) directly into the planning interface would reduce confusion.

2. How did you design your prototype(s) and subsequent user studies to address these questions?#

We created a hand-drawn prototype to quickly visualize the layout and core functionality without committing to implementation details. The design includes separate columns for fall and spring semesters, a section for major and general requirements, and a summary area displaying total credits and progress toward the major or concentration. I also incorporated labeled course categories (e.g., History, Spanish, Sociology) to simulate how users might browse and assign courses.

Then we asked the questions. The results they say is:

  • Degree requirements visibility: It’s helpful that the requirements and credits are shown together — I can quickly see if I’m on track. But it’s still a bit unclear exactly which specific courses or prerequisites I need.
  • Comparing across years: The fall/spring layout makes it easy to organize and see my workload across semesters. But it’s hard to compare different course options or plan alternatives.
  • Integrated requirement tracking: It does reduce confusion since everything is in one place. Still, I’m not fully sure if a course actually counts toward a requirement or not, which makes me a bit unsure.

3. What answers to your design questions did your efforts unveil?#

Our results show that the prototype successfully improved overall clarity and organization, but did not fully resolve key sources of confusion. Making degree requirements visible alongside credits helped users quickly understand their progress, confirming that integrating this information into the interface is valuable. The fall/spring layout also made it easier for users to plan across semesters and think about workload distribution.

However, users still struggled with more detailed decision-making, such as understanding prerequisite chains and knowing whether specific courses satisfy requirements. Additionally, while the interface reduced the need to track information mentally, it did not fully support comparing alternative course options or planning around uncertainty.

Overall, the prototype validated that we should focus on visibility and structure, but revealed the need for more detailed guidance, clearer requirement mapping, and better support for decision-making.
