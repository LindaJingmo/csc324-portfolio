Needfinding

April 26, 2026 · 4 min · Linda Jing

1. What techniques did you employ to discover users’ needs?#

I used a semi-structured interview combined with a workflow walkthrough. I asked open-ended questions about how the student plans courses and followed up by having him describe his actual step-by-step process, including how he searches, filters, and selects classes. I also asked under specific scenarios, such as what happens when he cannot enroll in a planned course, to uncover real pain points rather than just general opinions. This approach helped me capture both his behaviors and his underlying reasoning.

2. What did you learn about users’ needs from this process?#

I learned that course planning is largely constrained by requirements rather than driven by exploration, especially for structured programs like 3+2 engineering. The student relies heavily on manual processes and memory instead of structured tools, which creates inefficiencies and potential risks of missing requirements. A major challenge is uncertainty in course enrollment, including limited seats and prerequisite barriers, which often forces last-minute changes. He also expressed a need for clearer visibility into degree progress, better organization of requirements, and access to qualitative information such as professor quality.

3. What are the key implications for design?#

These findings suggest that a better system should focus on reducing uncertainty and cognitive load. We can provide clear tracking of degree progress, visualize prerequisite structures, and offer real-time signals about course availability and enrollment risk. It should also support more automated planning rather than requiring manual trial-and-error, while integrating both requirement data and qualitative insights to help students make more informed decisions.

Interview Notes#

Observation: How do you plan your four-year schedule?

“You do not have a lot of choices to be honest.” Since doing 3+2, the student must complete pre-engineering classes including 2 physics classes, 2 calc, linear algebra, intro chem, CSC151, and CSC161. He also needs to finish the econ major and complete 2 humanities classes. Beyond those requirements, he took political science, sociology, statistics, and religious studies.

His process: search by department name, use filters (300-level etc.), add all possible sections for the four classes he plans to take, then choose the final plan based on availability. When he did not get STA230, he looked over all possible courses and eventually took STA310.

Q&A

1. What year are you? Third year.

2. Major? Econ + CS, but won’t be able to finish CS since doing 3+2.

3. How confident were you with course planning before starting at Grinnell? Moderately confident, but with reservations. Concerned about too few seats for first-years and preregistration restrictions.

4. Where do you usually write your plan down, if anywhere? Mostly in his head. Some form of digital plan, but no strong attachment to a written 4-year document.

5. Without talking to your adviser, what was your idea for your 4-year plan? Initially did not want to take humanities. After talking to adviser, took Religious Studies.

6. What is the most frustrating part of planning your 4-year schedule? Course competitiveness. Barriers include too many students and too few seats, professor approval required, and registrar system constraints. Wishes tools were better — wants Rate My Professor integrated and clearer structure of the system.

7. Have you ever realized too late that a course didn’t count or had unknown prerequisites? No, but believes it could easily happen. Suggestion: add a major progress bar to prevent this.

8. How often do you change your 4-year plan? Rarely. Only changes when failing to get a prerequisite or getting blocked by enrollment issues.

9. How easy or hard is it to tell whether you’re on track to graduate? Very easy, but relies on memory of requirements and a mental checklist.

10. How does planning your 4-year schedule make you feel? Mixed emotions. Positive: “WOW, I made progress” — feels happy when scheduling works. Negative: “Oh no, being cut off” — extremely annoying when blocked.

11. Do you ever avoid planning because it feels overwhelming? No. If avoiding planning, it becomes even more overwhelming later.

12. What would make planning your 4-year schedule easier or less stressful?

  • Required course progress and status clearly shown
  • Rate My Professor integration
  • Better signaling of seat availability, prerequisite chains, and risk of being removed from a class
