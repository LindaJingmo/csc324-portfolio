Ideation

April 26, 2026 · 3 min · Linda Jing

What techniques did you use to discover solutions to users’ needs?#

We used persona-based ideation to explore solutions. By constructing three distinct user personas — a 3+2 engineering student, an undecided first-year, and a student who just changed their major — we were able to identify specific pain points and design targeted features for each case. Each persona represented a realistic situation that exposed a different gap in existing course planning tools.

Personas#

Persona 1 — Daniel: 3+2 Engineering Program

Daniel plans to spend only three years at Grinnell before transferring to an engineering school. He must satisfy both Grinnell’s degree requirements and the engineering school’s prerequisites within six semesters instead of eight.

Solution pursued: We designed a feature that lets users set a target completion semester for a major (e.g., “complete Physics in 6 semesters”). The planner then generates a schedule compressed to that timeline, helping students like Daniel meet both sets of requirements without manually counting backwards from a transfer deadline.

Persona 2 — Emma: Undecided First-Year

Emma has just started at Grinnell and has not chosen a major. She wants to explore course options while still making sure she is making progress toward graduation requirements.

Solution pursued: We added a fallback flow for undecided students. Instead of requiring a declared major, the planner asks what areas of interest the student has. Each time the auto-fill runs, it selects a major from those interest areas and generates a possible four-year plan. This lets Emma explore different paths concretely rather than just browsing course catalogs abstractly.

Persona 3 — Michael: Recent Major Change

Michael switched from Computer Science to Economics mid-college. He needs to know which courses he already took count toward his new major, and how to fill in the remaining semesters to graduate on time.

Solution pursued: We built a feature that lets users input courses they have already completed. The planner locks those courses in place, marks which requirements they satisfy in the new major, and then auto-fills only the remaining semesters. This gives Michael a clear picture of what transfers over and what he still needs, without starting from scratch.

What solution(s) did you ultimately pursue and why?#

We pursued all three features because each persona revealed a genuinely different constraint that a one-size-fits-all planner could not handle. A compressed timeline for transfer students, interest-based exploration for undecided students, and course-carry-over for major switchers all address real situations that students at Grinnell face. Together they make the planner flexible enough to serve students at very different stages of their academic journey, not just students with a declared four-year plan from day one.
